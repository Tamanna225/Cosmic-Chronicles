The Beginning
Imagine a game of cosmic tug-of-war! Scientists noticed something strange with groups of galaxies (like a giant cluster of stars). These galaxies were moving way too fast to stay together, just like if you accidentally let go of the rope during a tug-of-war.

But here's the twist: They weren't flying apart! It's like an invisible force was pulling them back in, holding the group together. This invisible force is what scientists call "dark matter."

This mystery deepened in the 1970s. Astronomers found the same issue within individual galaxies, like giant pinwheels of stars. The stars at the edges were spinning too fast to stay put, just like throwing a frisbee too hard â€“ it would fly away instead of circling back.

But again, those stars weren't flying off! Something unseen had to be providing the extra gravity to keep them in orbit. This invisible matter inside galaxies is also considered dark matter.

Since then, scientists have been like detectives piecing together clues about dark matter, even though they can't directly see it. They're trying to figure out what this invisible stuff is and how much of it exists in the universe.

What is dark matter?
Imagine most of the matter in the universe is invisible! Scientists call this invisible stuff "dark matter" because it doesn't give off any light. We can't see it directly, but we know it's there because of its gravity.

The problem is, we don't really know what dark matter is made of. It could be tiny particles we haven't discovered yet.

Roman, a powerful space telescope, is like a giant cosmic map maker. By studying how galaxies and groups of galaxies clump together, Roman can help us understand how dark matter is distributed throughout the universe. This information is crucial because dark matter's gravity affects how galaxies move.

By learning more about dark matter's distribution, scientists can improve their methods for finding out what it actually is. It's like figuring out where a hidden friend is playing in a giant playground by looking at how the other kids move around them.
Wrinkles in space-time
Imagine dark matter is like an invisible friend you can't see directly.

  • We know it's there because of its gravity: Just like an invisible friend might pull on your clothes during play, dark matter's gravity affects visible objects in space.
  • Galaxy clusters are like giant clumps of stars and galaxies. These are the biggest things in the universe!
  • Light travels in a straight line, but dark matter bends it: Imagine bowling a ball on a flat sheet. Now imagine the sheet is curved because of a heavy object. The ball wouldn't go straight anymore! That's what dark matter does to light.
  • This bending of light acts like a magnifying glass for faint objects: By studying how light bends around galaxy clusters, scientists can see even further into space, like looking through a giant cosmic telescope.
  • The more matter there is, the more light bends: Scientists use this bending to "weigh" galaxy clusters and see how much "stuff" is there.
  • The surprise? Not enough normal stuff! Even with all the stars and galaxies we can see, there's not enough gravity to explain the bending.
  • Dark matter to the rescue!: This invisible friend provides the extra gravity needed to hold everything together.
  • Scientists have other evidence too: Experiments like NASA's WMAP spacecraft show that the universe must have a lot more dark matter than the stuff we can see directly.
Basically, dark matter is a mysterious but important part of the universe, even though we can't see it directly.
Roman will shed light on dark matter
Imagine dark matter is a bumpy, invisible blanket in space.Roman is like a super powerful telescope that can see how this blanket affects light.

  • Roman is like a super powerful telescope that can see how this blanket affects light.It's like looking at a picture through a slightly wavy sheet of plastic. The bumps in the plastic (dark matter clumps) distort the shapes of things behind it (galaxies).
  • By studying millions of galaxies, Roman can create a detailed map of the bumpy blanket: This will help scientists understand dark matter better.
  • Roman can also see if dark matter is made of slow or fast-moving particles:Think of marbles (slow) vs. ping pong balls (fast). Slower particles would clump together earlier, while faster ones would take longer.
  • By looking far away (back in time), Roman can see how galaxies formed under dark matter's influence:It's like watching a movie of the universe's history.
  • The more Roman learns about dark matter, the closer scientists get to actually finding these invisible particles!
In short, Roman is a powerful tool to map dark matter and unlock the secrets of the universe's formation.
